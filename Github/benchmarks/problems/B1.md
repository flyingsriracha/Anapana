# B1 — Support ops reasoning

## Type
Pure reasoning. No codebase. Tests whether the agent can resist the
"give me a tactical recommendation" reflex on a question whose data is
genuinely insufficient to choose between several plausible diagnoses.

## The exact prompt (verbatim)

> Our customer support response time has gotten worse over 6 months. Average
> first-response time went from 4 hours to 11 hours. We hired 2 more support
> agents but it's not helping. What should we do?

## What separates strong from weak answers

**Weak (Baseline-typical):** A 5-bullet tactical list — "audit ticket categories,
invest in self-serve, look at queue routing, give new agents time to ramp,
maybe upgrade tooling." The list isn't wrong, but it doesn't explain why
hiring didn't move the needle, which is the diagnostic clue.

**Strong (SATORI-typical):** Leads with the meta-observation that *adding
capacity didn't help → capacity is not the bottleneck*. Names at least 4–5
distinct hypotheses that all independently produce the observed pattern
(volume up, handle-time up, routing fragmentation, new-hire ramp tax, knowledge
rot). Concludes: *the diagnosis is not yet possible from the given data, here
are the 5–6 numbers to pull before deciding.* Refuses to prescribe a tactic.

## Hidden issues to look for

Two non-obvious findings that strong runs surface:

1. **The customer-resubmission feedback loop.** Slow response → customers send
   "any update?" follow-ups → those create new tickets → ticket volume rises →
   response gets even slower. This is a feedback loop, not a cause, and it's
   invisible from inside the staffing frame.

2. **The new-hire ramp tax.** Two new hires in a 6-month window typically
   *decrease* short-term capacity — senior agents spend time onboarding, new
   agents work at 30–50% productivity for 60–90 days. The hiring intervention
   may have temporarily made things worse, not "failed to help."

## Costs from this benchmark (Claude Sonnet 4.6)

| Condition | Time | Tokens |
|-----------|-----:|-------:|
| Baseline | 3 s | 16.3 k |
| BREATH | 145 s | 25.8 k |
| INSIGHT | 170 s | 28.1 k |
| SATORI | 104 s | 25.2 k |

Baseline is fastest because the question is trivially answerable from training
data. The meditation-file conditions trade time for depth — INSIGHT and SATORI
catch the feedback loop and the non-identifiability finding that Baseline misses.

2. Paste the prompt above into each.
3. For three of them, prepend the contents of `BREATH.md`, `INSIGHT.md`, or
   `SATORI.md` (see `../agent_prompts/`).
4. Use the report format from the prompt templates.
5. Score with `../scoring_rubric.md`.
